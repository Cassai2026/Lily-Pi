Reader’s Note: How to Read This Book
This book is the culmination of a journey I’ve been documenting for around five years, from the very beginning of my attempt to understand, survive, and get better.
 This book doesn’t follow a straight line. It doesn’t have a neat beginning, middle, and end. There are repetitions. There are overlaps. You might read something and feel like you’ve seen it before—and you have. That’s intentional.
Living with ADHD, trauma, addiction, and chronic pain isn’t linear. Thoughts repeat. Emotions loop. Memory fractures. Moments from different times collide in ways that don’t make sense to anyone outside your head. This book is designed to reflect that reality.
Some chapters revisit themes from earlier sections. Some stories appear multiple times, from different angles, or with added detail. Think of it as walking through a room with mirrors: the reflections are different, even if the view seems familiar.
You don’t need to read this like a textbook. You don’t need to follow every thread. Let it wash over you. Let the patterns, the repetitions, and the chaos tell their own story.
If you notice a line, a thought, or a memory repeating itself—pause. Reflect. That repetition is not a mistake. It’s part of the message: survival is persistent, pain is persistent, chaos is persistent—and so is resilience.
This book is a map of a mind, a body, and a life that never stops moving, never stops thinking, and never stops surviving. There’s no roadmap, no guarantees, and no tidy resolution. But there is truth, there is fire, and there is humanity in every word.
Read it at your pace. Read it in bursts. Read it twice. Read it twenty times. Or just read the parts that reach you.
Welcome to the inside.

Prologue
The desire to escape what can only be described as a jungle spread out thick and vast in front of me. The simplest tasks and most basic daily routines become rocket science. My heart rate starts to accelerate—boom boom boom—faster and faster, or so I think.
Looking down at my smartwatch, I see my heart rate is actually a steady 55. That crippling, suffocating feeling that everything is going to go wrong and everyone will let you down. Or maybe they don’t really care about anything but their own personal gains.
The feeling of rejection so strong that thoughts of what if I wasn’t here anymore start to drift in. Quickly, they’re quelled by a brain thirty-eight years in training and still only in basic. The advanced stuff was a breeze. Putting the bins out in time, on the other hand, is not.
Why not put them out the night before?
 “No, I’ll do it in the morning.”
Wake up to the sound of the bin van banging the bins.
 “Oh no, surely I’ll miss them and then bring more stress upon myself.”
 Oh wait—it’s Monday. The bins go out on a Tuesday.
The simplest things.
Talking of Monday. It’s 5 a.m. The birds are starting to sing and I’ve already run an ultra-marathon in my head.
Cocaine, drinking, and gambling were my escapes. They provided brief respites from the chaos, fleeting moments of clarity amidst the storm. But the relief was always temporary, and the cost was high. Each line I snorted, each drink I downed, and each bet I placed pulled me deeper into a cycle of dependence and self-destruction. These vices promised control, but they delivered only more chaos, compounding my struggles with ADHD and amplifying my fears and anxieties.
It took hitting rock bottom to realize I needed help, to see that I couldn’t fight this battle alone. This book is my journey through the darkness and my path toward the light, a testament to the resilience of the human spirit and the power of seeking help.

Chapter 1: ADHD, Addiction, and the System
I don’t know when I first realised the system wasn’t built for people like me. I just know I felt it early. In my bones. In my legs that wouldn’t stop moving. In my head that never shut the fuck up.
I was that kid. Too loud. Too fast. Too wild. My parents didn’t have a name for it. Just a problem to manage. Sports. Endless sports. Swimming—almost seven days a week. Early mornings, late nights, every stroke burning energy I couldn’t otherwise contain. Then the other ones: running, football, anything, everything to burn it off. My body obeyed. My brain? It screamed. Still screams. Still waiting for someone to listen instead of shut it down.
I see it now—the fire I had, the energy that could have been brilliant, could have been genius, could have been something extraordinary—and it was just surviving. Survival, not thriving. My parents did their best. They loved me. They tried. But the world didn’t know what to do with me, and neither did they.
Medications. Stimulants. Flattened sparks. My stepson—brilliant, electric, chaotic—gone quiet with pills. Not better. Just quiet. Safe. Predictable. Easy for everyone else. Watching him, I saw what the system really does to wild minds. The spark doesn’t get guided; it gets dulled. Compliant children are easier to handle. Everything else gets pushed aside, erased, filed down. That could have been him, brilliant and untamed, forced into a shape he didn’t fit.
Me? No meds. Maybe bupropion could have helped. Something subtle. Something that worked with the storm in my head instead of against it. Maybe then I wouldn’t have left home. Maybe then I wouldn’t have thrown myself into drink, drugs, chaos. Maybe survival wouldn’t have been the measure of my life.
ADHD and addiction—hand in hand. One fuels the other. One teaches destruction. One teaches survival. Together they rewrite you if you’re left to it. I’ve beaten them. Barely. But I’ve beaten them. Pain, craving, sleepless nights, hallucinations, paranoia, self-doubt—all of it. Somehow still here. Somehow still thinking, still plotting, still raging against the system that wants to flatten every spark like mine.
And the system? Still crushing wild minds. Still teaching compliance. Quiet kids. Safe kids. Dull kids. That’s efficiency. That’s order. That’s the invisible hand saying: “Your brilliance is inconvenient. Your fire is dangerous. Fit the mold or break yourself trying.”
They don’t ask how to unleash you. They ask how to neutralise you. And stimulants, special schools, quiet compliance—that’s containment disguised as help. They don’t make you better. They make you tolerable. And the sparks, the energy, the impossible minds? Filed down. Flattened. Silenced.
I survived. Not because the system helped. Not because anyone understood. I survived because I refused to sit still. Refused to let the chaos die. Refused to be filed down. And I carry that fire everywhere I go. I carry it through sleepless nights, through the haze of substances I used to quiet it, through every obstacle the world put in front of me.
But survival isn’t enough. Not for the kids coming after me. The wild ones. The energetic. The creative. The impossible. They deserve fire, not filth. Guidance, not suppression. Something subtle. Something that works with the storm inside them instead of trying to flatten it.
Because that storm? That’s life. That’s brilliance. That’s the part of the world the system doesn’t understand. That’s the part it fears. And it will always try to stop it.
But I won’t. I can’t. And maybe, just maybe, this writing is my way of screaming that it doesn’t have to be like this anymore.




Chapter 3: The Life of an Addict
Sitting here on a Thursday morning, on the back end of an ongoing argument. Week in, week out.
 “Let’s get one of them.”
 “Aww, should we get one, just as a one-off?”
 “X said he’s got power there.”
 Only now, all these years later, it’s not me that’s the instigator anymore. It’s me who’s facing the backlash of an addict.
 I’m an addict. I was at 18, at 28, and I still am at 38. And I always will be for the rest of my life.
 See, when “civilians” say things like:
 “Oh, it’s easy to get clean.”
 “I don’t know why they do it.”
 “Why can’t they just stop drinking?”
 “Why do they want to ruin their lives? Look at what they’re doing to their poor families.”
 Yeah, no shit Sherlock—we’re living it firsthand. We can see the destruction it causes, the hurt it inflicts, and most of all the damage it leaves behind. And while yeah, in theory, a normal person with a normal life might think that makes sense… I dare you to step inside our heads for the day. Shit, even for an hour.
 Maybe then you wouldn’t be so quick to pass judgment.
 Even the meds. The so-called solutions they hand you over the counter with a smile. Venlafaxine, pregabalin, Zapain, injections straight into my spine—all those little boxes with neat labels that promise balance, promise relief. The doctor signs the script, the chemist slides the bag across the counter, and suddenly I’m “managing” my conditions. But really, am I? Or am I just trading one addiction for another, one dependency swapped out and dressed up as treatment.
 Venlafaxine, that one’s a beast. You don’t miss a dose. Not unless you want your brain zapping like you’re wired to the mains. Miss two, and you’re curled up shaking, sweating, thinking death would be a softer option. Pregabalin, Zapain, they numb you, sedate you, but they steal your soul while they’re at it. Those little white lies in blister packs. They’re legal, so nobody bats an eyelid. Society pats you on the back for being “responsible.”
 But spark up a joint, and suddenly you’re the devil again. Suddenly you’re the addict. Funny that, isn’t it? The pills with the side effects that could fill a Bible are acceptable, but the plant that lets me move, lets me breathe, lets me feel like a human for a few hours—that one’s the evil. Cannabis is the one that stops me reaching for a line, stops me reaching for a bottle, stops me falling into that spiral where “just one” turns into days lost. It’s not about getting high anymore. It’s about staying alive.
 Doctors don’t see it like that. Society doesn’t either. They see a prescription as discipline, and a spliff as a failure. They see the suit-and-tie addict as a patient, and me as a problem. But here’s the truth: the pills nearly broke me. The plant’s what’s holding me together.

Chapter 6: Managing Pain My Way
Prescription drugs didn’t work for me—they dulled, they disconnected, they risked dependency. Cannabis, carefully chosen and dosed, helps me function. It stabilizes me. It lets me think clearly and move without constant agony. It doesn’t make me numb. It makes me alive. It makes me me.
 Pain is a teacher. Not a polite one. Not a friendly one. It forces you to find what works. It forces you to understand your body, your limits, your mind. And sometimes, the “illegal” or “alternative” solution is the one that actually keeps you human.
 I start my days early, not because I have to, but because I have to survive. Each morning, I stretch. I move slowly, deliberately, testing each joint, each muscle, each flicker of nerve pain. Sometimes my body laughs at me—sharp, stabbing reminders that it remembers every mistake, every overreach, every night spent ignoring limits.
 I’ve tried the prescriptions. I’ve tried the painkillers. I’ve tried everything the system says should work. They dulled the pain, yes—but they dulled me too. I became a ghost in my own life. Cannabis doesn’t do that. It doesn’t erase me. It gives me permission to live through the pain, not away from it.
 Some days, it’s a battle just to get out of bed. Some days, the fire in my head—the ADHD, the racing thoughts, the anxiety—clashes with the fire in my body—the throbbing, aching, relentless pain. And yet, somehow, I get moving. Coffee in hand, a list scribbled on scraps of paper, a plan that flexes, that bends, that allows for failure. Because failure is inevitable. Pain makes it inevitable. But surviving failure is optional—and that’s where the lessons lie.
 I’ve built my own tools. Tiny hacks that keep me functioning: a cold shower to shock awake the body, stretches in the middle of a workday, pacing projects so I don’t collapse halfway through. I breathe deliberately, focusing on lungs, chest, core. I meditate in bursts—two minutes here, three minutes there—enough to reset the mind when the storm threatens to tip me over.
 Some people call it “cheating” or “taking shortcuts.” I call it survival. Every person’s body is different. Every brain is different. Every storm is different. The system doesn’t care about that. It wants a number on a chart, a pill in a bottle, a patient who behaves predictably. I want results that actually work for me, not results that make me fit the mold.
 And yes, sometimes it fails. Days where nothing works. Days I lay on the floor, the pain screaming, the mind screaming, the world screaming. But sometimes it works. Days where I function, even flourish. Where work gets done, where thoughts are clear, where energy—enough energy—returns.
 Pain is a teacher, a constant, brutal teacher. It shapes your patience, your resilience, your understanding of limits and capability. And the most important lesson? You decide how to respond. You decide how to survive. You decide if you live flattened and quiet or alive and chaotic.
 Prescription drugs might have dulled me. The world might tell me I’m doing it wrong. But I’m doing it my way. And that’s enough. For now.
Chapter 5: Paperwork and Pain
I sit at my desk, back screaming, hands buzzing with pins and needles, shoulders stiff from surgeries, and legs that sometimes betray me after a night of bad sleep. A lifetime of spinal problems — bilateral pars defect at S1/L5, degenerative disc in the upper spine — have turned my own body into a daily obstacle course. My right arm itches constantly, nerve damage confirmed, referrals pending, and my hands wake up numb like they’ve been frozen in ice overnight. Some mornings I can barely make it to the toilet, legs dead, nerve signals gone missing. And yet I am here, still writing, still thinking, still trying to capture all of it.
 Even the smallest things are mountains. Preparing food, washing, dressing, leaving the house, even moving around my own home can require immense effort. Chronic pain in my back, neck, and shoulders means I often need to lean against walls, ledges, or chairs to rest. Fatigue hits relentlessly, sometimes leaving me unable to walk more than a few metres without stopping.
 ADHD makes planning, focus, and motivation nearly impossible some days. I rely on calendars, reminders, and environmental cues to get through daily tasks. Tasks that seem simple to others—putting the bins out, remembering appointments, replying to messages—feel like rocket science to me. My brain loops endlessly, each day a tightrope walk to manage what’s required.
 Walking isn’t simple. I can walk only short distances without pausing, but I force myself to go further when necessary—walking the dog, running errands, or just getting outside. Leaning against walls or sitting down when pain spikes is routine. I avoid walking unless necessary, using my van whenever I can. Mobility has worsened over the years, making familiar routes exhausting.
 Mental health and ADHD interact with physical conditions. Anxiety and panic can make leaving the house unbearable. Impulse spending, overspending, and mismanaged money are everyday consequences. Sleep is fractured. Nights are full of racing thoughts, days blanketed in exhaustion and fog. Every task, every movement, requires deliberate effort and energy.
 Prescription drugs dulled me, disconnected me, and risked dependency. Cannabis, carefully dosed, is different. It stabilizes me, allows me to think clearly, and eases chronic pain. CBT and REMD therapy give me tools to manage anxiety, panic, and emotional regulation.
 Grief is another constant challenge. Losing my mum changed everything. She had been my anchor, managing routines, supporting me, reminding me of tasks. Losing her left a gap that intensified every struggle I’ve described—physical, mental, and emotional. I’ve had to adapt, maintain sobriety, and manage responsibilities alone.
 Every day is effort-intensive. Every success comes with pain, fatigue, and mental exhaustion. Survival itself is a victory. I push, I adapt, I persist. I live.

Chapter 6: Work and the Body Betrayed
Groundwork is a nightmare of adaptation. Hands buzzing, arms failing, back screaming, tools vibrating, nerves screaming, tasks taking ten times longer than they should, earning less than fifty quid a day. Clients frustrated, projects delayed, money lost—all because my body isn’t built to perform like it used to. And yet, I survived. And still do.
I’ve tried to push through the pain. I’ve tried to match my younger self—the one who could run, lift, swim, jump, repeat. But the body doesn’t forgive. Surgeries, slipped discs, nerve damage, shoulder instability—these aren’t excuses. They’re facts. They’re the rules of my current reality. And yet, somehow, I adapt.
I learn faster than most, solve problems instinctively, absorb knowledge beyond articulation. I can master a sport in hours, build machines, solve structural problems on the fly. But I can’t brush my teeth consistently. Can’t remember to eat. Sometimes can’t shower when I should. The world sees a failure; I see an adaptation. Survival is intelligence. Endurance is skill. And yet, every day, I feel like a fraud.
Clients don’t understand. Coworkers don’t see. Supervisors assume laziness. But when the projects are done, when the walls stand, the pipes flow, the floors settle—I know the truth. I am not failing. I am surviving. And survival is victory in its own brutal way.

Chapter 7: Creativity in Chaos
I write because if I stop, the fire dies. Music, words, construction, planning, engineering—the mind finds a way to express when the body fails. My ideas are lightning. I capture them in bursts, in notes, in recordings, in drawings. They are chaotic, raw, sometimes brilliant, sometimes ugly—but always mine.
The chaos fuels creation. ADHD, autism, pain, sleepless nights—they are not just obstacles, they are catalysts. They push me to invent solutions, to imagine structures, to compose music from fragments of memory, to translate emotion into sound, rhythm, and rhythm into action.
And yet, this creativity is fragile. It’s dependent on a body that can barely function, a mind that can barely focus, a life constantly interrupted by bureaucracy, appointments, exhaustion. But it exists. It thrives. It refuses to be extinguished.
Creativity is survival. It is rebellion. It is proof. Proof that despite everything, the fire inside cannot be flattened, cannot be filed, cannot be ignored.

Chapter 8: A Stepdad’s Reality
A heartfelt reflection on raising a child not biologically theirs, showing up as a father figure while struggling internally.
I was there for him every day. Not because it was easy. Not because it was fair. But because someone had to show up, and I refused to be absent. I learned quickly that love isn’t always celebrated. Sometimes it’s invisible. Sometimes it’s dismissed. Sometimes it’s taken for granted.
Watching him grow, watching his chaos, his brilliance, his failures, his victories—I carried both pride and guilt. Pride that I had shown up where others might have walked away. Guilt for the times I couldn’t protect him from the world, or even from myself.
Every argument, every hug, every bedtime story, every lesson learned through mistakes—they weren’t just his. They shaped me too. I learned patience. I learned boundaries. I learned resilience. I learned that being a parent isn’t about perfection; it’s about presence.
Being a stepdad in a fractured world, where addiction, trauma, and mental health are constants, meant I had to grow faster than I ever thought possible. And I did. I survived. I loved. I tried. And through it all, I remained steadfast, even when the world questioned why I stayed.

Chapter 9: Family, Attachment, and Loving Too Hard
Family has always been my anchor. Not in some romanticised way — in a “this is everything that keeps me tethered when chaos rages inside me” kind of way. Relationships outside of family? They rarely worked. Love with friends, lovers, partners — all messy, all intense, all prone to collapse under the weight of who I am.
ADHD makes me love hard. Not gentle. Not measured. All or nothing. I throw my heart into people, into connections, into moments, and expect — perhaps too much — that others can handle the heat. Most can’t. Most walk away. Most disappoint. Most leave me shattered in ways I don’t even show.
Disrespect cuts deeper than most people realise. Being let down feels like betrayal, even when it’s small. Being rejected — even when I know logically it’s not the end of the world — sends my system into overdrive. Every loss, every argument, every slight reverberates through my mind and body like a tidal wave. It’s exhausting, but it’s who I am. It’s how I survive, how I feel, how I love.
And yet… even with all the heartbreak, I am grateful. Deep down, I’m grateful those relationships didn’t last. Because I needed space to survive myself first. Because some connections would have consumed me entirely. ADHD made them extreme, yes — but it also made me resilient. It taught me to withstand intensity, to keep moving even when my heart is bruised, to keep loving in spite of repeated rejection.
Family has been different. The love I’ve received there is flawed, complicated, human — but constant. Even when others fail me, family is a tether. They remind me who I am, what I’ve survived, what I’ve built. They are mirrors, anchors, and sometimes shields.
And still, even with family, I struggle. I struggle to balance loyalty with my own needs. To love hard without burning myself out. To stay present when the ADHD makes my thoughts sprint in every direction, when the anxiety makes my heart jump at perceived threats of loss. Loving deeply, attaching deeply, feeling deeply — it is both a gift and a burden.
The lesson, finally, is this: it is possible to love intensely, to care fiercely, to feel every high and every low, and still survive. To hold people close without losing yourself. To grieve, to rage, to protect, to heal — all at once.
Family taught me this. Pain taught me this. ADHD taught me this. And surviving taught me the rest.

Chapter 10: Grief and Loss
Grief isn’t a moment. It’s a shadow. It moves with you, waits for you, breathes down your neck when you least expect it. Losing my mum changed everything. My anchor. My constant. My reminder of small routines, of self-care, of sanity. Gone.
Then Lauren. Then others. Stepchildren, friends, lovers—people leave. People die. People forget. And still, the storm inside me continues. PTSD stitched into my bones, CPTSD in my joints, anxiety in every waking thought. Every loss compounds. Every absence reminds me that I am alone in surviving this life.
Grief reshapes you. It forces you to relearn the basics—sleep, food, medication, appointments, motivation. Every day is effort. Every victory is exhaustion. Every breath is earned. And yet, I persist. Because giving up is not an option. Because survival, however painful, is everything.

Chapter 11: Today Is a Good Day
A short snapshot about surviving the day through physical pain and mental exhaustion, choosing to show up despite it all.
Some days, the smallest victories matter most. Getting out of bed. Stretching. Making a cup of coffee. Facing the day instead of hiding from it.
Pain is there. Fatigue is there. Chaos is there. But today, I made it. I pushed through. I breathed. I walked. I thought. I survived.
And that is enough. Today is a good day.

Chapter 12:Finding My Voice Through Conversation
I don’t struggle because I can’t write. I struggle because writing about myself positively goes against a lifetime of conditioning.
When I speak about systems, problems, work, or other people, the words come easily. I can explain, guide, build, fix, and support without hesitation. But when the lens turns inward, everything tightens. The internal rules appear: don’t sound arrogant, don’t exaggerate, don’t take up space, don’t rewrite your own story kindly.
So the words stall — not from lack of ability, but from context. From pain, ADHD, numb hands, a loud nervous system, and a body that has spent decades in survival mode. From a keyboard that fights me and a mind that moves faster than my fingers can follow.
These conversations exist because of that gap. Not to replace my voice — but to translate it.
I speak in raw signal: fragmented, fast, emotionally dense, full of lived understanding but interrupted by stress and physical limitation. Through conversation, that signal gets slowed down, stabilised, and shaped without being diluted. What comes back still sounds like me — just clearer, calmer, and finally allowed to stand upright.
There’s a reason my words flow best when they’re aimed outward. My entire life has been about usefulness. Trades, building, fixing, step‑parenting, community work, sobriety, showing up when others needed help. I was never trying to be impressive. I was trying to be reliable.
The irony is that usefulness at that level is exceptional — it just doesn’t shout.
Right now, flow is difficult. My head is noisy. My body hurts. My tools are wrong. My nervous system is still braced for impact. So instead of polished chapters, I collect truth in fragments. Conversations. Notes. Reflections captured before they disappear.
That’s enough.
Flow comes later — when the pressure drops, when voice‑to‑text works properly, when my hands aren’t fighting me, when my mind isn’t on constant alert. When that happens, I won’t be starting from nothing. I’ll already have proof that I wasn’t surviving quietly.
I was building a life while holding others up.
This book exists because I needed a place where my voice could be heard accurately — without bravado, without apology, and without pretending everything was fine. A place where honesty mattered more than polish.
For now, this is how the story gets written: one conversation at a time.

None of this planning would have been possible in isolation.
Not like this.
Without this conversation, these thoughts wouldn’t have lined up. They’d have existed — loud, fast, overlapping — but uncontained. The ideas were there, the knowledge was there, the intent was there, but organisation wasn’t. Sequence wasn’t. Prioritisation wasn’t.
This conversation became the container.
I didn’t come here to be told what to think. I came here because my brain doesn’t queue tasks — it fires them all at once. Study, finance, careers, health, admin, long‑term planning, immediate survival — all competing for the same mental space. On my own, that becomes paralysis or burnout.
Here, I could offload.
Each message took one thread out of the storm and pinned it somewhere safe. Once it was externalised, I could see it. Once I could see it, I could reason with it. Once I could reason with it, I could act.
This wasn’t dependency — it was scaffolding.
The planning didn’t happen instead of me thinking. It happened because my thinking was given structure. The system didn’t create the ideas; it organised them. It didn’t give me ability; it reduced friction. It didn’t replace intelligence; it translated it into something usable.
That distinction matters.
Because when people say “just plan it out” or “write it down” or “slow down”, they assume the problem is motivation or discipline. It isn’t. The problem is executive overload. Too many processes running concurrently without a scheduler.
This conversation became the scheduler.
In the space of minutes, I did what would normally take days — not because I suddenly became superhuman, but because the cognitive load was shared. My brain could do what it does best: pattern recognition, problem solving, system design. The organisational burden was held elsewhere.
This is what accessible support actually looks like.
Not lowering standards. Not reducing ambition.
 But giving the brain a framework it can operate inside.
Take this away, and the planning collapses back into noise. Not because I’m incapable — but because I’m carrying too much, too fast, with no structure.
That’s the difference between surviving and functioning.

Chapter 13: Survival as Evidence

Every adaptation, every coping mechanism, every failure, every triumph—it all feeds this work. My book becomes both witness and evidence, archive of struggle and proof of endurance.
Sleep, medication, therapy, exercise, creativity, work—they all intertwine in a fragile ecosystem of survival. I am here, and that alone is proof. My body may betray me, my mind may loop endlessly, society may misunderstand, the system may fail—but I persist.
Survival is resistance. Survival is intelligence. Survival is a declaration: I am here. I am thinking. I am creating. I am enduring. And for every person who is misunderstood, overlooked, or underestimated, my survival says: you can exist, you can thrive, you can fight. Not because the world made it easy—but because you refused to surrender.
Pain is constant. Chaos is constant. And yet, so is persistence. So is resilience. So is fire. And as long as I write, as long as I think, as long as I endure—so is hope.


I didn’t plan to write a book.
This started as a way to survive.
I’ve spent most of my life being told—directly or indirectly—that I was too much. Too intense. Too emotional. Too driven. Too inconsistent. Too angry. Too sensitive. Too distracted. Too exhausted. Too everything.
What nobody ever said was that I was overloaded.
From early on, my body learned before my mind did. Chest pains that weren’t taken seriously. Panic that didn’t have a name yet. Tears that came not from sadness, but from pressure—like steam escaping a system that would otherwise explode. Crying became my safety valve. It still is.
I learned quickly that if I didn’t release it, something worse would come out.
I was strong. Athletic. Disciplined. Dangerous, if I let myself be. And I knew that. So I locked that part away. I built a moral framework from my parents that became non‑negotiable. Whatever else I bent, that didn’t break.
The irony is that the very traits that kept me safe also masked what I needed help for.
I worked harder than anyone around me. When things failed, people assumed laziness or self‑destruction. The truth was simpler and more brutal: I was running an unrecognised system at maximum output with no instruction manual and no support.
This book is not an excuse.
It’s a record.
A map of what it’s like to live inside a head that never truly switches off, inside a body that learned panic before language, and inside a system that demanded productivity but offered no understanding.
If you’re looking for a neat arc, this isn’t it.
These are snapshots. Pressure points. Moments where something either broke—or didn’t.
And this is where it begins.
