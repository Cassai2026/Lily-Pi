PROLOGUE

Translation
There are faces everywhere.
Some are in the room.
Some appear as projections.
Some are present through headsets, rendered as still figures listening from different corners of the world.
A nine-year-old in Nairobi.
A seventy-two-year-old engineer in Osaka.
A teenager in São Paulo.
A grandmother in Glasgow.
Languages overlap, but no one waits for translation.
The system handles that.
Subtitles flicker quietly beneath each speaker. Voices convert in real time. Accents dissolve into clarity.
No one is excluded because of language.
But that isn’t the most important translation happening.
A young boy speaks in fragments — thoughts colliding before finishing.
The AI structures it gently. Not correcting him. Clarifying him.
An academic responds in dense terminology.
The system renders it simple.
Language flows both ways.
Neuro to linear.
Linear to neuro.
Culture to culture.
No one is reduced.
No one is elevated.
Someone asks me,
“When did this start?”
I don’t answer with technology.
I answer with a memory.
I was five.
And I said, “I’ll try harder.”

CHAPTER ONE

Fitting In
I was five when I first realised that knowing things doesn’t mean you belong.
The classroom was small. Low tables. Plastic chairs. The carpet had coloured blocks stitched into it — red, blue, green — like it was trying to organise chaos by colour alone.
I didn’t feel behind.
That’s the strange part.
I felt full.
When the teacher asked a question, I already had three answers — and two more questions forming behind them.
If she asked, “What happens when it rains?” I wasn’t thinking, “It gets wet.”
I was thinking about clouds.
Why they hold water.
Where the water was before it was in the cloud.
Why puddles disappear.
Whether the sea ever empties.
How boats float if they’re heavy.
All of that would fire in seconds.
And while I was still processing that chain, the class had already moved on to drawing a raindrop.
So when I spoke, it sounded unrelated.
Too much.
Too far.
Too fast.
I wasn’t behind.
I was out of sync.
And that’s lonelier.
Because if you’re behind, someone can help you catch up.
If you’re out of sync, you don’t know what to fix.
The teacher would say, “Sit still.”
And I would try.
But my body buzzed.
Not hyper.
Not naughty.
Just charged.
Like there was too much electricity for such a small frame.
When I was told to try harder, I meant it.
What I meant was:
I don’t know what I’m doing wrong.
I want to be good.
Tell me how to make my brain quieter.
But five-year-olds don’t say that.
They say, “I’ll try harder.”
And adults hear effort.
They don’t hear confusion.
________________________________________

OUT OF SYNC
It started small.
I interrupted.
Not to dominate.
To connect.
The teacher would be mid-sentence and my brain would already be at the end. I’d jump in, excited, finishing her thought or offering the next one.
But interruption isn’t collaboration in a classroom.
It’s disruption.
“Let me finish.”
“Put your hand up.”
“Stop talking.”
I learned quickly that my timing was wrong.
Then came correction.
One day, I corrected a teacher.
Not smugly.
Not aggressively.
Just factually.
There was a pause.
Correcting a teacher isn’t collaboration.
It’s challenge.
Later, I drew the monkey.
Stick body.
Long tail.
Exaggerated features.
Seven-year-old humour.
Monkey see, monkey do.
Someone saw it.
Laughter.
Then silence.
The paper was taken.
I remember the look more than the words.
Disappointment.
I wasn’t trying to mock.
I was trying to survive boredom.
But in that moment, I became:
Cheeky.
Disrespectful.
Difficult.
And once a child is labelled, behaviour bends around the label.
Not because they want to.
Because they feel it.
________________________________________

WHEN PASSION BECAME “TOO MUCH”
It didn’t hurt when I was told to stop talking.
It hurt when I cared.
When something interested me, it wasn’t mild.
It was everything.
I didn’t dip my toes into curiosity.
I dived.
If I liked something, I wanted to know how it worked.
Where it came from.
Who built it.
Why it existed.
What would happen if it failed.
And when I spoke about it, I spoke fast.
Excited.
Layered.
Thoughts stacking as I formed them.
To me, it felt alive.
To others, it felt intense.
“Calm down.”
“It’s not that deep.”
“Why are you getting so worked up?”
I wasn’t angry.
I was enthusiastic.
But intensity is often misread as aggression.
So I adapted.
Sometimes I shrank it.
Sometimes I hid it.
Sometimes I got defensive.
Sometimes I doubled down.
Excited → misunderstood → adjust → frustrated → push → misunderstood again.
It wasn’t conscious.
It was survival.
And slowly, without noticing, I started translating myself before anyone else had the chance to misunderstand me.
Not maturity.
Compression.

CHAPTER TWO

The Cost of Staying Still
I don’t remember becoming exhausted.
I remember always being tired.
Not sleepy.
Tired in the way your body feels after holding tension too long.
From a young age, I was moving.
Sports.
Swimming.
Running.
Anything that let energy out instead of trapping it in.
Physical exhaustion made sense.
Mental exhaustion didn’t.
Sitting still all day feels passive from the outside.
But when your body wants to move and your brain is firing constantly, sitting still is work.
You don’t relax into a chair.
You hold yourself there.
“Sit still.”
“Stop fidgeting.”
“Calm down.”
Repeated daily, they become internal commands.
Don’t move.
Don’t overflow.
Don’t be too much.
Swimming made sense.
In water, I wasn’t excessive.
I was aligned.
You moved.
You breathed.
You pushed.
You felt the burn and then the settle.
By the time I was seven I was already swimming kilometres.
Not because anyone forced me.
Because water was the one place everything lined up.
Movement.
Breath.
Effort.
The noise in my head didn’t disappear.
But it organised itself.
Swimming didn’t slow my mind.
It gave it rhythm.
By thirteen the training had expanded.
Running.
Cycling.
Swimming.
Triathlon.
Long sessions.
Endurance sessions.
Not casually either.
Competing.
By fifteen I was racing adults.
Some of them were army.
I beat them.
I became Manchester junior triathlon champion.
The prize was a flying lesson.
No one called that intensity a disorder.
When intensity wins races, it’s called discipline.
But alignment only lasted as long as the session.
Then it was back to compression.
When exhaustion becomes baseline, irritation isn’t far behind.

Chapter Three

The Years I Started Believing Them
Secondary school doesn’t feel like a sudden shift when you’re in it.
It feels like the same room, just louder.
Bigger corridors.
Stronger lights.
Higher expectations.
By the time I moved into early secondary school, I was already tired.
Already compressing.
Already translating myself before speaking.
But something subtle changed.
In primary school, being “too much” was irritating.
In secondary school, it became defining.
Teachers didn’t know your history.
They met you fresh.
And if the first impression was interruption, intensity, unfinished homework, or restless energy — that became your file.
“Capable but inconsistent.”
“Bright but distracted.”
“Needs to focus.”
Potential followed me here too.
But now it sounded different.
Less hopeful.
More warning.
I was still trying to fit in.
That’s important.
I didn’t wake up rebellious.
I didn’t decide to reject the system.
I tried.
I sat still.
I copied notes.
I attempted to care about things that felt detached from anything real.
But effort without alignment drains you quickly.
And when you’re already exhausted, the smallest friction feels amplified.
________________________________________
Performance
Around this time, I learned something new.
Confidence can be performed.
If you speak quickly enough, joke sharply enough, move boldly enough — people assume certainty.
So sometimes I leaned into that.
If I was going to be seen as intense anyway, I’d own it.
Answer quickly.
Challenge casually.
Laugh it off.
It worked socially sometimes.
But inside, nothing had settled.
Because performance doesn’t fix misalignment.
It hides it.
And hiding takes energy.
________________________________________
The Shift in Identity
Somewhere in those early secondary years, something dangerous happened.
I started believing parts of what I’d been told.
Maybe I am inconsistent.
Maybe I don’t apply myself.
Maybe I’m just not disciplined enough.
That’s the turning point for a lot of kids.
Not when they rebel.
When they internalise.
Because once you start believing the narrative, you stop questioning the structure.
You question yourself.
And questioning yourself is heavier than questioning a system.
________________________________________
Exhaustion Becomes Irritation
Exhaustion doesn’t stay quiet forever.
It mutates.
If you’re tired long enough, you become irritable.
If you’re constantly compressing yourself, something pushes back.
Not dramatically.
Not explosively.
Just small cracks.
Sarcasm sharpens.
Patience shortens.
Tolerance thins.
And when that shows, it reinforces the label.
“Attitude problem.”
But attitude wasn’t the origin.
Depletion was.
________________________________________
Trying Harder, Again
I remember trying again.
Revision timetables.
Last-minute focus.
Bursts of hyper-concentration.
And here’s the confusing part:
When something finally clicked, it clicked hard.
I could understand complex ideas quickly.
I could see patterns others missed.
I could perform well in moments.
Which made inconsistency look like choice.
If you can do it sometimes, why not all the time?
That’s the misunderstanding.
An AuDHD nervous system isn’t driven by discipline alone.
It’s driven by interest, relevance, and regulation.
Without those, output collapses.
Not intelligence.
Output.
But to teachers, output is what matters.
So the narrative deepened.
Potential.
But wasted.

Chapter Four
The Quiet Isolation
It wasn’t one moment.
That’s what made it harder to recognise.
There wasn’t a dramatic scene where I stood alone in a corridor and realised I didn’t belong.
It was quieter than that.
It was constant.
Isolation, for me, didn’t look like being excluded.
It looked like being present but out of sync.
I had friends.
I laughed.
I trained.
I showed up.
But internally, there was always a gap.
In conversations, I’d notice it.
The way my mind would move faster or sideways.
The way I’d connect ideas no one else seemed to be following.
The way jokes would land differently depending on how much I explained them.
I learned to trim thoughts before sharing them.
Shorten.
Simplify.
Reduce.
Not because I wanted to.
Because it made interaction smoother.
But when you repeatedly edit yourself to fit the room, something subtle happens.
You begin to wonder which version of you is real.
The compressed one?
Or the one you keep inside?
And that question doesn’t feel dramatic when you’re twelve or thirteen.
It just hums quietly in the background.
Maybe I just don’t fit anywhere.
Not as self-pity.
Not as rebellion.
As observation.
I didn’t feel behind.
I didn’t feel superior.
I felt… misaligned.
Like I was tuned to a slightly different frequency.
Close enough to hear the music.
Not close enough to match the rhythm.
________________________________________
The Performance Deepens
By this stage, performance wasn’t optional.
It was strategic.
If I led with humour, people relaxed.
If I led with intensity, they braced.
If I led with curiosity, they sometimes disengaged.
So I adjusted.
I became sharper in some rooms.
Quieter in others.
Louder when needed.
Detached when safer.
But every adjustment required awareness.
And awareness requires energy.
By the end of a school day, I wasn’t just tired from lessons.
I was tired from calibrating.
Who do I need to be here?
How much is too much?
How little is too little?
No one teaches you that skill.
You learn it to survive socially.

________________________________________

The Invisible Cost
The difficult thing about constant misalignment is that it doesn’t trigger immediate crisis.
It erodes slowly.
You don’t wake up thinking, I’m broken.
You wake up thinking, I need to try harder.
Again.
And again.
And again.
Until trying harder becomes exhausting.
And exhaustion begins looking for relief.
________________________________________

Around that time my grandmother died.
She had lived with us since the day I was born.
Not the kind of grandmother you visited occasionally.
She was simply there.
Part of the house.
Part of the routine.
Part of the background of childhood.
In many ways she was a second mum.
She was ninety-three when she passed.
To most people that sounds like a full life.
And it was.
But when someone has been present every day of your childhood, their absence changes the atmosphere of a home.
The rooms look the same.
The routine continues.
But something underneath it shifts.
My mum noticed the change in me afterwards.
Even years later she would mention it to my best friend.
Not blaming anything.
Just recognising that something had tilted.

That was the edge.
The point where coping starts becoming something else.
The point where exhaustion begins looking for an off-switch.


Chapter Five
Work As Relief 

Before I ever reached for anything destructive, I reached for work.
Work made sense.
Work had edges.
Work had rules that were physical, not abstract.
If you moved something from here to there, it moved.
If you fixed something, it stayed fixed.
If you showed up, you were useful.
School often felt like theory without traction.
Work felt like traction.
So I worked.
Early.
Often.
Hard.
It wasn’t about money at first.
It was about regulation.
When you’re physically engaged, your mind has somewhere to go.
When your body is tired, it rests more easily.
When a task is concrete, you don’t have to interpret social cues or compress enthusiasm.
You just do the job.
There’s something deeply stabilising about physical labour when your nervous system runs hot.
It channels intensity.
It gives it purpose.
I didn’t see it as coping.
I saw it as momentum.
If I was moving, I wasn’t stuck in my head.
If I was building, I wasn’t compressing.
If I was useful, I wasn’t “too much.”

________________________________________

A Different Classroom

On site, no one told me to sit still.
No one misread energy as aggression.
Intensity wasn’t disruptive.
It was productive.
If something needed solving, I focused.
Not halfway.
Not inconsistently.
Fully.
Hyperfocus, when pointed at a real problem, looks like competence.
No one calls it disorder when it produces results.
They call it dedication.
That was confusing.
Because in one environment, the same brain was problematic.
In another, it was valuable.
So which was true?
Was I incapable of discipline?
Or was I wired for applied structure?
Work didn’t judge that question.
It rewarded output.
And output felt grounding.

________________________________________

The Trap Inside the Solution
But here’s the part that took longer to understand.
Work regulated me.
It didn’t resolve me.
I could exhaust myself physically and still feel internally unsettled.
I could finish a long day satisfied and still wake up wired.
Because the core misalignment wasn’t about productivity.
It was about translation.
Work reduced friction.
It didn’t explain it.
So I worked more.
If a little helped, more must help more.
That’s a familiar pattern in high-intensity nervous systems.
Push harder.
Move faster.
Stay occupied.
Silence is louder when you stop.
So I avoided stopping.



Chapter 6

Structure
Before the parties fully took over, there was something else.
I decided I was going to join the Marines.
That decision wasn’t random.
It was clarity.
For someone whose nervous system ran hot, the idea of discipline wasn’t restrictive.
It was attractive.
Order.
Hierarchy.
Physical demand.
Clear rules.
No ambiguity.
No emotional translation.
You move when told.
You push when told.
You endure.
There’s comfort in defined edges when your internal world feels diffuse.
So I trained.
Hard.
Physically, it made sense.
The exhaustion felt earned.
The focus felt purposeful.
For the first time, intensity wasn’t excessive.
It was required.
There was something stabilising about that.
Not ego.
Not bravado.
Containment.

________________________________________

Chapter 7
The Fracture
Then life interrupted.
A breakup.
And breakups at that age aren’t minor emotional inconveniences.
They destabilise identity.
Especially when your regulation isn’t stable to begin with.
What had been focused became fractured.
What had been directed became diffuse.
Training didn’t stop immediately.
But something shifted internally.
When the emotional anchor moved, the structure didn’t feel as solid.
And that’s when the party life accelerated.
Not because discipline failed.
Because emotional destabilisation exposed how fragile the balance was.
Raving became more frequent.
Drinking increased.
Cocaine entered.
Here’s the truth without glamour:
Cocaine doesn’t slow the nervous system.
It sharpens it.
For someone already wired fast, it feels powerful at first.
Confident.
Focused.
Socially fluid.
But it’s borrowed clarity.
And borrowed clarity charges interest.

________________________________________

Chapter 8
Turning the Volume Down
Work helped.
Training helped.
But neither quieted the noise.
When the day ended, the mind didn’t.
It was still fast.
Still layered.
Still branching.
Raves.
Music that didn’t ask you to sit still.
Rooms where intensity wasn’t too much.
Environments where loud wasn’t disruptive — it was normal.
For someone whose nervous system ran hot, that felt aligned.
The bass wasn’t chaotic.
It was rhythmic.
Predictable.
Physical.
You didn’t have to translate yourself.
You moved.
MDMA entered around the same time.
And here’s the truth without drama:
It didn’t feel destructive at first.
It felt quiet.
For the first time, the constant internal calibration softened.
Edges smoothed.
Tension eased.
The background hum reduced.
It wasn’t about escape.
It was about relief.
Alcohol followed.
Pills followed.
And the strange part was this:
I still showed up.
All night raving.
Early morning home.
Straight to work.
Functioning.
From the outside, it looked like stamina.
From the inside, it was regulation by chemistry.
I wasn’t chasing thrill.
I was chasing stillness.

________________________________________

Chapter 9
When Chaos Becomes Routine
There wasn’t a single crack.
There wasn’t a morning where I looked in the mirror and thought,
“This has to stop.”
It didn’t feel like destruction.
It felt like balance.
Party at night.
Sleep during the day.
Work when needed.
Repeat.
It wasn’t random.
It was structured chaos.
I did what I had to do to make my life possible.
The rave scene wasn’t just noise and lights.
It was community.
It was rhythm.
It was shared intensity.
No one told you to calm down in a room built for volume.
No one misread energy as aggression.
You were allowed to be loud.
Then you came down.
Slept.
Showed up.
Earned.
Built.
From the outside, it looked like stamina.
From the inside, it was a life engineered around regulation.
And because I was still functioning, it didn’t register as collapse.
It registered as lifestyle.
You’re not chasing chaos.
You’re chasing equilibrium.

Chapter 10
Interruption
There wasn’t a dramatic moment of clarity.
No mirror scene.
No speech.
No cinematic collapse.
There was interruption.
Sixteen years ago now.
When you build your life around movement — work, nights, rhythm, noise — interruption feels unnatural.
It wasn’t chaos that led to it.
It was accumulation.
Momentum without pause.
I still worked.
Still showed up.
Still functioned.
That’s what makes it harder to recognise.
Functioning doesn’t mean aligned.
It just means surviving.
The arrest wasn’t identity.
It was consequence.
Not evil.
Not rebellion.
Consequence of a life engineered around regulation instead of resolution.
For the first time in years, everything stopped.
Work paused.
Nights paused.
Momentum paused.
And when movement stops, silence arrives.
And silence is louder than any rave.
The arrest didn’t fix me.
It broke the illusion of control.
And once illusion cracks, reflection becomes possible.
Not instantly.
But inevitably.

Chapter 11

Too Much
There’s a misconception that working around death makes you wise.
Sometimes it just makes you overwhelmed.
I was young.
Still regulating poorly.
Still carrying unresolved intensity.
Still trying to understand myself.
Death every day is not neutral.
It forces reflection before you’re ready.
And reflection without support can spiral.
It wasn’t that I didn’t care.
It was that I cared too much.
And I didn’t know how to metabolise it.
So the grounding phase ended.
The overload phase began.
And overload looks similar to restlessness.
Which is how Magaluf entered.
Not as rebellion.
As relief.


Chapter 12
The Weight of Silence
The funeral industry is not loud.
There are no flashing lights.
No basslines.
No overstimulation.
There is air.
Still air.
And still air can feel heavier than noise ever did.
When my uncle gave me the opportunity to work with him, it didn’t arrive wrapped in speeches.
There was no dramatic intervention.
It was quiet.
An offer.
A chance.
Not shouted.
Not forced.
Just placed in front of me.
I don’t think I understood the magnitude of it at the time.
But I understood one thing:
This wasn’t noise.
This was gravity.
________________________________________
Death Changes the Room
The first time you stand in a room with someone who has died, something recalibrates inside you.
There’s no performance there.
No exaggeration.
No ego.
The room feels honest.
Not dramatic.
Honest.
All the intensity that once filled my nights had no relevance in that space.
No one cared how loud you were.
No one cared how sharp your wit was.
No one cared how fast your brain ran.
They cared about dignity.
Care.
Respect.
Presence.
And presence is something you can’t fake in that environment.
You either show up fully.
Or you don’t belong there.
________________________________________
A Different Kind of Responsibility
Working in funerals is not physically explosive work.
It’s controlled.
Precise.
Measured.
You move slowly.
You speak carefully.
You consider impact.
Every action carries weight.
And for someone whose life had been built around intensity and movement, that shift was profound.
It demanded regulation from within.
No chemicals.
No noise.
No escape.
Just you.
Standing still.
Doing something that matters in a way that can’t be faked.
________________________________________
Perspective
When you carry someone who has died, arguments feel smaller.
Ego feels smaller.
Impulses feel smaller.
You realise something simple and brutal:
Time is finite.
And for the first time in a long time, that thought didn’t feel abstract.
It felt close.
The party life had been about intensity.
The arrest had been interruption.
This was confrontation.
Not with law.
With mortality.
And mortality doesn’t argue with you.
It doesn’t negotiate.
It doesn’t care about your potential.
It only asks:
What are you doing with the time you have?
________________________________________
The Quiet Influence
My uncle didn’t preach.
He didn’t shame me.
He didn’t drag my past into every conversation.
He gave me responsibility.
That’s different.
Responsibility says:
I believe you can carry this.
And belief, when given quietly, can rewire a person more than punishment ever could.
I began moving differently.
Speaking differently.
Thinking differently.
Not instantly.
But gradually.
The noise reduced.
Not because it was forced out.
Because something heavier replaced it.
Purpose.
Not grand purpose.
Not world-changing purpose.
Just immediate, tangible responsibility.
And responsibility stabilises differently than adrenaline ever could.

Chapter 13
Escape
Magaluf wasn’t freedom.
It was avoidance.
Sun.
Noise.
Excess.
No quiet rooms.
It felt lighter.
Because nothing there demanded depth.
No families grieving.
No existential weight.
No silent rooms.
Just stimulation.
But you don’t escape your nervous system by changing geography.
You just change scenery.
And intensity follows you.
The year passed in noise.
But something subtle shifted.
The fun didn’t feel as sharp.
The nights didn’t regulate as cleanly.
The pattern was repeating.
And repetition eventually exposes itself.

Chapter 14
Escape Velocity

It wasn’t planned.
It wasn’t strategic.
It wasn’t a life decision built over weeks.
It was spontaneous.
I got a payment from a crash one night.
Booked a flight the next day.
Left.
That’s how fast it happened.
No long conversations.
No gradual drift.
No farewell tour.
Impulse + opportunity = movement.
When your nervous system is overloaded, stillness feels dangerous.
Movement feels safe.
So I moved.
Magaluf wasn’t  a dream I’d been building toward.
It was distance.
Distance from funeral rooms.
Distance from weight.
Distance from silence.
The speed of the decision tells you everything.
There was no reflection phase.
There was acceleration.
Because reflection would have required sitting still with what was building inside me.
And sitting still had never been my strength.
________________________________________
The Pattern Revealed
Looking back, that moment says more about me than the arrest ever did.
The arrest was consequence.
Magaluf was choice.
Fast.
Reactive.
Clean break.
And on the surface, it worked.
New place.
New rhythm.
New identity.
But underneath, nothing fundamental had shifted.
Intensity followed.
Regulation still depended on extremes.
Noise still masked silence.
You can outrun a room.
You can’t outrun yourself.



Chapter 15
Conception
When I came back from Magaluf, I wasn’t enlightened.
I wasn’t fixed.
I was just… back.
Same nervous system.
Same intensity.
Same patterns circling quietly underneath.
But then something happened that restructured everything.
Jaxson was conceived.
And responsibility stops being philosophical when it becomes biological.
It’s no longer:
“What am I doing with my life?”
It becomes:
“What am I building for someone else?”
That shift is different.
It’s not ego.
It’s not pride.
It’s gravity.
The kind that doesn’t ask permission.
________________________________________

Not loudly.

A second chance.
And this time, it felt different.
Because now I wasn’t stabilising just for myself.

The Paint Surgeon.
Not a dream scribbled on paper.
Not a fantasy.
A unit.
Tools.
Responsibility.
Invoices.
Reputation.
Structure.

One hundred hours a week.
Not because someone forced me.
Because intensity finally had direction.
________________________________________
Intensity Reframed
This is important.
The same brain that was “too much” in classrooms…
The same nervous system that chased regulation through chaos…
The same energy that escalated in Magaluf…
Now it had a container.
When hyperfocus points at something constructive, it doesn’t look like disorder.
It looks like obsession.
And obsession, in business, can look like drive.
Long nights.
Early mornings.
Learning everything.

Building something from nothing.
For the first time, exhaustion felt earned in a different way.
Not depleted.
Invested.
________________________________________
Legacy
When you’re about to become a father, legacy stops being ego-driven.

I didn’t want Jaxson to inherit chaos.

Architecture.

Now I was building my own.
Not to rebel.
To prove something could work.

Chapter 16
Functional but Unresolved
I was building.
The Paint Surgeon wasn’t just a job. It was a life raft.
A container for all the intensity I hadn’t figured out how to direct.
Forty hours in funerals.
Sixty in the shop.
One hundred hours a week.
I was moving.
And that’s how you know you’re functional.
If you can work, show up, earn, pay bills, keep the world from spinning off course,
you tell yourself it's fine.
The world doesn’t measure whether you’re happy or whole.
It measures if you keep going.
And I kept going.
But here’s the thing about functional systems:
They work.
Until they don’t.
________________________________________
The Pressure Underneath
On the surface, everything looked like it should.
A small business.
A son on the way.
Stability.
But deep down, the patterns remained.
The same nervous system that demanded movement didn’t pause when success arrived.
It didn’t suddenly quiet.
It didn’t suddenly heal.
Work felt grounding, yes.
But it didn’t resolve the internal noise.
I still lived with the same intensity, but now it was buried under practical necessity.
I didn’t stop to ask whether I was truly balanced.
I didn’t stop to question whether I was building a life I actually wanted.
I was still calibrating myself, just faster.
I had learned to survive — to perform, to show up, to push forward — but surviving isn’t living.
And deep down, the question remained:
What am I really building?
________________________________________
The Unspoken
I remember the first time I looked at Jaxson and thought:
“I want to get this right.”
But the “right” didn’t feel tangible.
The desire to be stable felt heavier when faced with responsibility.
I wanted to be the father who showed up, who worked hard, who provided.
But I also wanted to give him more than that.
Not just the grind.
Not just survival.
Something I hadn’t had.
Something real.
And when that thought hit, it didn’t feel like clarity.
It felt like a new weight.
What was I doing if I wasn’t building a life with a foundation that didn’t shake?
That’s when I knew:
I wasn’t healed.
I was functioning.
But unresolved.
________________________________________
The Silence Between
I wasn’t asking the big questions.
I wasn’t pausing long enough to reflect.
I was still moving.
The noise was quieter.
But it wasn’t gone.
I had found a temporary answer, a distraction, in the intensity of work.
But the question beneath it was different.
It wasn’t: “Can I keep moving?”
It was: “What am I moving towards?”
I wasn’t living inside the answers.
I was still running.
________________________________________
The Shift to Stability
That’s when the reflection process began, without me realising it.
The ground was steady, yes.
But the question was always underneath.
This wasn’t about just “being okay.”
It wasn’t about just being functional.
It was about being real.
And that’s when the shift would eventually happen.
I’d realise that stability, while important, wasn’t the whole answer.
Because I was still functioning at a cost.
A mental cost.
An emotional cost.
And that cost, while invisible, was starting to show up.
